### Provisional title of proposed research grant or fellowship application you plan to submit in 2026

Evaluating fit for purpose modelling during infectious disease outbreaks

### Please provide a BRIEF summary of the proposed work, including a succinct background, aims, objectives, and methods. (Max 2000 characters, including spaces).

During infectious disease outbreaks, quantitative modelling can be a crucial tool for interpreting uncertain evidence. Recent modelling research has seen a surge of both interest and impact, but the pace of development has not been matched by similarly rigorous evaluation. Typically, evaluation might include quantitative predictive accuracy, and/or qualitative expert judgement of model credibility. However, there are no common standards for these methods, and these are not consistent, reproducible, or transparently reported. Meanwhile, modelling work used to inform policy is typically neither reported nor evaluated. 

This raises both scientific and democratic concerns, and risks undermining the legitimacy of modelling work. Relatedly, fields such as climate science that apply modelling to public decision-making have started to explore the concept of “fit for purpose” model evaluation. I aim to advance the professionalisation of infectious disease modelling work, building a framework for evaluation from the assessment of model fitness, purpose, and infrastructure.

Research strategy
1. To identify model “fitness” for real-time forecasting, with a set of baseline standards for quantitative evaluation. Using a dataset of >100 modelling teams’ publicly available forecasts, developing methods for the statistical decomposition of forecast errors, combined with expert elicitation to identify quality standards across model structures and settings. 
2. To characterise the purposes to which infectious disease modelling is applied in public health contexts globally. Using bibliometric analysis, cross-sectional surveys, and interviews to map modelling capacity, opportunity, and motivation in policy settings.
3. To improve infrastructure for fit-for-purpose modelling during outbreak response. Using the UK’s use of modelling advisory groups in national pandemic response as a case study, for using both quantitative and qualitative evaluation methods developed above.

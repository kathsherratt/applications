# JUNIPER Fellowship 

## Background

JUNIPER Fellowship scheme is now open for applications. The fellowship is available for up to two years and is aimed at supporting early career epidemic modellers to develop leadership skills and grow their research network as they transition to becoming an independent researcher.

### Support for JUNIPER Fellows
Will be paired with a JUNIPER member from a different institution (someone who could potentially be a sponsor for a fellowship).
Financial support to cover travel and accommodation costs to enable visit to their JUNIPER member partner (or other relevant UK academic) in-person. This could be a single long visit or multiple short visits depending on individual circumstances.
Eligible to request a letter of support from JUNIPER for grant and fellowship applications.
Eligible to apply for financial support to run a JUNIPER workshop.
Regular meetings with the Senior Scientific Programme Manager and input to the strategic direction of JUNIPER through recommendations to the Management Board.
Opportunity to work with our science writers (Plus Magazine).

### Eligibility
Should NOT have a permanent post, or be eligible to be Principle Investigator on a UKRI funded grant.
Should be UK based with a current post at a research institute (University), and is expected to be at a UK research institute for the duration of the fellowship.
Must be an affiliate of JUNIPER.

## Application

### Please provide a short overview of your research interests.*

My core research interest is the use of rapid evidence synthesis at the science-policy interface. I am specifically interested in evaluating collaborative “team science” approaches to modelling work during outbreaks of infectious disease. This includes methods for model comparison and combination (ensemble modelling); combining scientific judgements through expert elicitation and consensus forming; and evaluating the costs and benefits of such synthesis for actionable, evidence-informed decision making. My background focuses on real-time epidemiological analysis and forecasting during outbreak response. I previously worked at the Wellcome Trust before training in epidemiology. I then worked on the COVID-19 response, contributing to the UK’s Scientific Advisory Group for Emergencies and developing and evaluating modelling infrastructure for the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control. I am now building from this and transitioning to an independent research career that bridges the critical evaluation of science-policy interfaces with quantitative modelling work.

### As part of the fellowship, we expect JUNIPER fellows to lead research activities within JUNIPER. Please outline what you would do in JUNIPER as a fellow.*

I propose leading a theme of work based around modelling evaluation. As a fellow, I would aim to work across the JUNIPER network to understand common challenges in evaluating modelling outputs, outcomes, and processes, and explore the potential for a consensus around best practices. 

We currently lack strong guidance or field-wide standards for model evaluation. Modelling work is typically quantitatively evaluated based on predictive accuracy, and/or qualitatively evaluated using expert judgement. However, these methods may not be appropriate to the aims of the modelling work, reproducible, or transparently reported. This lack of a documented common framework for evaluation both exacerbates the uncertainty of modelling work, and risks compromising trust and use in decision support. This becomes especially important in times of rapid evidence production and synthesis, including real-time outbreak response. At the same time, evaluation might extend beyond internal model credibility. For example, evaluations of modelling at the interface of ecology and policy often add the dimensions of “relevance” (e.g. to a policy objective), and “legitimacy” (e.g. ethical and appropriate stakeholder involvement). 

I suggest exploring practices of evaluation in epidemic modelling, as an opportunity to bring together and build the JUNIPER network. This could be achieved through a JUNIPER affiliated workshop, potentially supplemented with other (likely qualitative) methods for identifying evaluation practices. I have relevant experience in expert elicitation, focus groups, and the Delphi method of consensus building, as well as conducting surveys and interviews. Focusing on this theme builds on the strengths of JUNIPER’s collaborative approach, where identifying current practices in evaluation requires both a diversity of modelling expertise, and a highly co-operative setting that emphasises mutual respect.  Results from this work could include: a community of practice for sharing and developing methods for evaluation; published work reporting current practices; and/or the development of a consensus considering appropriate forms of evaluation at different points and for different purposes in epidemic modelling.

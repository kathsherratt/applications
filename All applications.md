#### Infectious disease modelling at the science-policy interface

The offer of infectious disease modelling is to synthesise epidemiological insight into actionable guidance for public health. Computational modelling may serve multiple functions: synthesising sparse data to create real-time situational awareness; projecting epidemic trajectories to inform resource allocation; or exploring the population impact of public health interventions (Hesterbeek 2019, Morgan 2019). The last thirty years has seen the growth of infectious disease modelling in both capacity and methodological sophistication (Metcalf et al., 2015, Becker, Funk). This has been celebrated as critical to better informing public health (Biggerstaff). In some areas, such as vaccine allocation or health technology assessment, infectious disease modelling has become a routine and relied upon source of decision support (). 

Yet this opportunity also presents costs, and challenges when meeting the realities of political public health decision-making. During the 2001 foot-and-mouth disease outbreak, models played a contested role in justifying culling policies, generating "destructive tension" between modellers and field practitioners (Mansley et al., 2011). The 2003 SARS and 2009 H1N1 outbreaks prompted further waves of reflection on integrating models into decision-making (Glasser et al., 2011; Van Kerkhove & Ferguson, 2012). 

#### Evaluation at the interface

George Box's observation that "all models are wrong, but some are useful" (Box, 1979\) has become an aphorism. Evaluating technical correctness requires different research questions and methods than establishing overall utility in contributing to decision-making or public health outcomes. 

While technical accuracy may in principle be assessed independently, for example through proper scoring rules (Gneiting), evaluating usefulness requires engaging with the decision-making context, considering decision counterfactuals, and understanding the values guiding policy choices (Parkhurst & Abeysinghe, 2016, Oliver 2024).

This distinction has received increasing attention through the concept of "fit-for-purpose" modelling. A fit-for-purpose model is appropriate to the specific decision context, rather than optimised for technical performance alone. 

Boden and McKendrick proposed an ethical framework for model-based policymaking emphasising transparency about limitations, appropriate uncertainty characterisation, and consideration of who bears the costs of model-informed decisions (Boden & McKendrick, 2017). These perspectives suggest that evaluation of modelling for policy requires methods beyond model verification or calculations of predictive accuracy. These methods that remain underdeveloped in the infectious disease field. 

While technical evaluation has a well-established methodological basis, policy evaluation lacks equivalent standards, leaving a significant gap in our ability to assess whether modelling efforts genuinely support better decisions.

#### COVID-19 as a catalyst

The COVID-19 pandemic brought infectious disease modelling to sudden public salience. Modelling groups previously operating within academic structures were redeployed to national response efforts, with outputs consumed by media, politicians, and the public (Atkinson et al., 2022; Medley, 2022). This visibility accompanied rapid institutional adaptation. Scientific advisory structures expanded significantly, while new collaborative infrastructures emerged (Medley, ?Clapham, Reich et al).

The pandemic resurfaced familiar challenges.  Under substantial pressure, model development and use was constrained by limited capacity, data gaps, and competing priorities, even as demand increased (Owek et al., 2024). Modellers navigated between providing clear guidance and communicating uncertainty (Atkinson et al., 2022), and faced fresh scrutiny on the ethical and social obligations of conducting politically influential research (Inquiry 2025, Zachreson 2023). The experiences of COVID-19 prompted community reflection, and clear areas for improvement in order to prepare for future outbreak responses (Imperial/WHO, Kucharski, Le Rutte, 2024, Sherratt et al., 2024). These efforts represent an opportunity for positive change, but have largely been happening in isolation. We identified a need to compare methodology for such evaluative work, and consolidate resulting insights in the use of modelling in policy processes.

We currently lack strong guidance or field-wide standards for model evaluation. For example (drawing on evaluation of modelling in the ecological sciences), potential themes for evaluation could include: credibility, e.g. the internal and external validity and reliability of modelling work; relevance, e.g. the use of modelling in public health decision making; and legitimacy, e.g. ethical evaluation of modelling processes and stakeholder involvement. 

There is no clear standard for what comprises a “good” model. While “all models are wrong”, models may be wrong in many different ways. While forecasting models may be explicitly evaluated based on accuracy against observed data, this is not their only use. Predictive accuracy is often considered inappropriate for the type of scenario projections designed to provide policy insights. 

At the same time, evaluation might extend beyond model credibility. A widely used framework in ecological modelling suggests also considering “relevance”, for example to a policy objective, and “legitimacy”, for example ethical and appropriate stakeholder involvement. 

This becomes especially important in times of rapid evidence production and synthesis, including real-time outbreak response.  It is particularly challenging to compare among multiple models without objective standards on which to assess model quality. 

At best, modelling work may be evaluated based on quantitative predictive accuracy and/or qualitative expert judgement. However, this is not often transparently reported or reproducible. At worst, a lack of evaluation risks creating unintentional harms by validating modelling evidence of inappropriate quality for the intended use. 

Other policy-facing modelling research suggests potential frameworks for model evaluation. For example, a widely adopted structure for evaluation in ecology is that of “credibility, relevance, legitimacy”. “Credibility” indicates internal model validity (for example, accuracy against observed data); “relevance” its usefulness for addressing a specific (policy) question. The dimension of “legitimacy” concerns structural and ethical considerations in the process of modelling, such as appropriate public participation.

# Case for Support: What is the marginal value of additional modelling for epidemic intelligence during emergencies?

## Summary

This research programme evaluates how the quality of epidemic intelligence changes with the quantity of modelling work available during emergency response. COVID-19 revealed both the appetite for modelling advice and the absence of evidence about which specific modelling perspectives add value. This research evaluates the quality of additional modelling in terms of the quality of evidence (evaluating model agreement and error); and quality of advice (establishing the boundaries of professional responsibility). I propose contextualising this research by tracing modelling work during UK emergency response. Expected outputs include reporting guidelines for model evaluation (PRISMA equivalent), meta-analysis and software to support the evaluation of new modelling work, facilitation protocols for rapid multi-model synthesis, and training resources that embed evaluation and ethics into modeller education. The long-term aim is to further the professionalisation of real-time epidemic modelling work: from fragmented, ad hoc gap-filling into a recognised discipline with explicit quality evaluation.

### Aims and research questions

This research programme investigates how the quality of epidemic intelligence changes with the quantity of modelling work available during emergency response. The central question is: what is the marginal value of adding another modelling perspective when an outbreak is unfolding? The answer matters because every health system faces a constraint on specialist capacity, and the pandemic revealed a global appetite for modelling advice that far exceeded the supply of credible modellers.

I investigate this through two core research questions, contextualised by a detailed historical case study.

**Evidence quality and evaluation.** When multiple independent models are run on the same data, how much information does each additional model add? Which signals are redundantly captured, and which aspects of the outbreak are genuinely illuminated by different approaches?

**Advice quality and professional responsibility.** How do modelling groups synthesise evidence into policy recommendations, and what are the ethical and operational boundaries of that responsibility?

**Case study: UK emergency response.** Does the structure that emerged during COVID-19 represent a durable, principled model for other health systems, or did it emerge from contingent circumstances?

### Expected outcomes

The research will produce outputs in three categories.

**Evidence quality and evaluation.** Reporting guidelines for forecast evaluation (analogous to PRISMA), a Delphi consensus on standard evaluation metrics, a meta-analysis toolkit for combining results across forecasting systems, and empirical characterisation of redundancy and complementarity in contemporary forecasting efforts.

**Advice quality and professional responsibility.** A facilitation protocol for rapid multi-model synthesis in emergencies; evidence on which forecast attributes actually influence policy decision-making; and guidance on the professional responsibilities and ethical boundaries of modellers who advise on policy.

**Case study: UK emergency response.** A detailed reconstruction of SPI-M's history, operations, and accuracy; empirical assessment of what it achieved and which aspects were calibrated; and lessons for designing fit-for-purpose advisory architecture.

Collectively, these outputs support professionalisation of real-time epidemic modelling as a recognised discipline with explicit protocols, quality standards, and continuous evaluation, rather than ad hoc gap-filling activity.

### Background and motivation

The COVID-19 pandemic normalised epidemic forecasting at the highest levels of policy-making. What was historically a peripheral activity in academic epidemiology became organisational infrastructure. The Science Pandemic Influenza Group on Modelling (SPI-M) moved from coordinating a handful of academic models to the heart of the UK response. In parallel, forecasting hubs formed internationally, aggregating dozens of independent models into ensemble predictions. By 2021, decision-makers could choose between multiple sources of modelling advice, each representing substantial analytical effort.

This scale of effort prompts the question: does more modelling improve the quality of epidemic intelligence? Three gaps in understanding emerge: whether we know how to evaluate the evidence that models produce; whether we understand how to synthesise that evidence into responsible policy advice; and whether the structure that emerged during COVID-19 represents a durable model for future emergencies.

**Evidence quality and evaluation.** COVID-19 produced dozens of forecasts of the same quantities (case trajectories, hospital demand, variant prevalence), yet there is no consensus on what "good" forecast performance looks like in a policy context. Standard evaluation metrics exist, but comparing models fairly—when they are built on different assumptions, fitted to different data, or designed for different decision problems—requires clarity on standards that is currently absent. Without such clarity, decisions about which models to commission, how to weight them, and when to ignore outliers are made by intuition or precedent rather than evidence.

**Advice quality and professional responsibility.** How do modelling groups synthesise evidence to produce policy advice? Real-time modelling teams operate under time pressure with incomplete information. The way they deliberate, disagree, and reach consensus has consequences for the advice that filters into policy, yet this process is almost entirely undocumented. Who sits around the table, which models get credence, how disagreements are resolved—these operational details shape the advice, but are rarely examined. Without understanding which group structures and facilitation methods produce better advice, capacity-building and training rely on imitation rather than evidence about what actually works.

**Case study: UK emergency response.** Does the structure that emerged during COVID-19 represent a durable, principled model for other health systems, or did SPI-M's particular form emerge from contingent circumstances? SPI-M's effectiveness, and the appropriate scope and scale of such advisory structures, cannot be determined without historical evaluation of what it actually achieved and how its design related to the outcomes it produced.

### Approach

The research programme is organised around two core research questions, contextualised through a detailed historical case study.

**Evidence quality and evaluation.** I will conduct a formal Delphi study with forecasting practitioners and evaluation experts to build consensus on standard reporting metrics—a professional norming activity establishing what the field agrees constitutes rigorous evaluation. The output is reporting guidelines comparable to PRISMA, enabling consistent comparison across forecasts and contexts. Concurrently, I will analyse models across forecasting hubs by collecting standardised data from open-source systems, conducting principal component analysis to characterise error divergence, and developing methods to compare the contribution of different error sources (data quality, assumptions, outbreak stage, variant type). The result is a map of model genealogy: which models capture identical logic, and which are genuinely independent?

**Advice quality and professional responsibility.** I will map the network-of-networks to show who collaborates across institutions and systems, and how this architecture changed since COVID-19. Bibliometric analysis will reveal where expertise is clustered and sparse. I will conduct a structured trial of facilitation methods for multi-model synthesis with existing modelling groups, using expert elicitation methods (SHELF) to test different approaches and measure groupthink and information loss. A discrete choice experiment with the public health intelligence workforce will identify which forecast attributes actually influence decision-making. From these, I will develop a practical facilitation protocol for rapid multi-model synthesis and guidance on the professional and ethical boundaries of such work.

**Case study: UK emergency response.** I will reconstruct the history of SPI-M using documentary research (inquiries, Hansard, freedom of information) and interviews with participants. I will then evaluate its accuracy retrospectively, mining consensus statements for probabilistic language and assessing calibration and bias. This produces an empirical and historical account of what SPI-M achieved, which aspects were accurate, and how its effectiveness changed with complexity and uncertainty. From this, I can extract guidance on fit-for-purpose advisory architecture: whether SPI-M's structure represents a principled model for other health systems or emerged from contingency.

### Challenges and how they will be addressed

Three challenges are anticipated. First, accessing the granular data on SPI-M's operations and predictions requires both historical retrieval and cooperation from participants. This is addressed through early engagement with the organisations involved (SPI-M members, UKHSA) and established freedom of information pathways. Second, heterogeneity across forecasting systems means that direct comparison of Hub models requires careful standardisation of data collection and explicit handling of methodological differences. This is addressed through collaboration with the Hubverse team and development of a transparent protocol for pooling and comparative analysis. Third, the ethical dimensions of modelling in policy are complex and contested. Embedding them into training requires sustained engagement with the modelling community and clarity about what "ethical" means in real-time contexts. This is addressed by establishing a community of interest early in the project, ensuring that ethical frameworks are developed collaboratively rather than imposed.

### Key milestones

Year 1 will establish foundations: scoping reviews of existing evaluation practices, initiation of the Delphi consensus process, beginning of SPI-M documentary research and interview programme, and initial network mapping. By month 12, the reporting guidelines will be drafted and the SPI-M historical narrative will be taking shape.

Year 2 will focus on empirical testing: completion of the Hub comparison, running the facilitation trials with existing modelling groups, and conducting the discrete choice experiment with public health teams. The focus is on generating primary empirical data rather than purely synthesising existing work.

Year 3 brings software development and synthesis: implementation of baseline models package, finalisation of the facilitation protocol, and completion of the retrospective calibration analysis of SPI-M consensus statements.

Year 4 focuses on dissemination: publication of guidelines, release of software, deployment of the facilitation protocol with UKHSA for testing in a real-time setting, and development of training resources embedding evaluation and ethics.

### Key collaborators and research environment

The work is based at CMMID, LSHTM, with visits to UMass Amherst (collaboration on forecast evaluation methods and baseline models), University of Pennsylvania (network analysis and facilitation methods), ECDC Stockholm (European forecasting context), and WHO Pandemic Hub Berlin (global perspective on advisory structures).

The Epinowcast, Epiverse, and the Hubverse communities are essential partners for technical development and implementation of evaluation tools. Nick Reich at UMass Amherst and Kat Shea, Justin Lessler, and Emily Howerton at University of Pennsylvania are established collaborators on forecast evaluation and comparative methods. The ECDC brings expertise in European surveillance and forecasting and integrating new methods into European surveillance systems. 

Within the UK, collaborators include UKHSA, the LSHTM-Imperial HPRU Health Analytics and Modelling, and JUNIPER, which coordinates modelling for pandemic preparedness. The WHO Pandemic Hub and Crisis Hub in Berlin provide global context on how modelling advice is structured across health systems. The Australia scenario modelling consortium is interested in learning from the historical approach to SPI-M and implementing prospective evaluation of policy-relevant modelling in an alternative context. The Health in Humanitarian Crisis Centre at LSHTM provides links to humanitarian modelling contexts.
